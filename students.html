Students

The Challenge

Even though the Hispanic population ranks second in population size, Hispanics are growing in College Admissions, yet are far behind other ethnicities when it comes to College Degrees awarded. This will be demonstrated below with statistics from The National Center for Educational Research (2018) and reports from MIT DataUSA (2019).

There are many contributing factors to this phenomenon of low retention and persistence, which have been touched on in numerous higher education publications. I want to draw on just a few here:

  1. Many, if not most, of the Hispanic high school graduates are first-generation students. While high school is a priority for their parents, college is often considered an unaffordable luxury, rather than an investment.
  2. Most have to navigate the labyrinth of college admissions processes alone, as their parents in their majority speak Spanish as their first language (22% of Chicago Hispanics speak Spanish as their primary language, while the population of Chicagoâ€™s Hispanics reach 29%, [Datausa.io]).
  3. Some high school graduates have DACA status, so they have stressful uncertainties regarding continuance in the country, and do not have access to Federal Aid and MAP grants. They are reluctant to take private loans, unsure if they would be able to secure legal employment to pay the debt.
  4. Some come from fragmented families, where they are made to provide economically for their siblings and remaining parent, balancing school, work, and family.
  5. Some have suspended coursework for indefinite periods of time to attend to family crisis.
  6. Many begin their families early, which means they are providing for their own children, larger family unit, and studies with limited emotional and financial resources. Studies take a back burner, and retention is lower, and persistence is a challenge.

The Gap

We can see from the following DataUSA graph that the population of Chicago can practically be divided into three thirds, where Whites (32%) are only three points above Hispanics (29%) and Blacks (29%).

However, the distribution of degrees granted in Chicago is disproportionally high for Whites (46%) and significantly low (statistically speaking) for Blacks (19%) and Hispanics (17%) (MIT DataUSA, 2019).

In a similar way, Black communities disproportionally rank first in poverty rates (37%), with more than 10 points above Hispanics (24%) and Whites (23%).

These disadvantages are aggravated in the case of women, who between 18 to 35 years old constitute the population most affected by poverty.
